We can agree on several important points—especially that salvation is through faith in Christ alone and not by works (Romans 3:28). I also appreciate the emphasis that baptism is not what saves us, but rather is a testimony of what Christ has already done. That’s an essential distinction.

1. The Biblical Mode of Baptism—Immersion
You’re right that the mode of baptism is a picture. And because of that, it matters how we portray that picture. The Greek word for “baptize” (βαπτίζω) literally means “to immerse” or “to dip under.” Every New Testament example of baptism after Christ’s resurrection shows immersion (e.g., Acts 8:38–39; Romans 6:3–4). The symbolism—death, burial, and resurrection with Christ—is beautifully and clearly displayed through immersion. Substituting another mode changes that picture.

2. The Timing of Baptism Teachings
You mentioned that the examples I may have cited were pre-atonement. That’s a fair observation, but the New Testament’s consistent post-resurrection pattern of believer’s baptism by immersion continues after Christ’s atonement. The Great Commission (Matthew 28:19) was given after the resurrection, and the apostles obeyed it by baptizing new believers through immersion.

3. Exceptions and Early Church Practice
It’s true that some writings from early church history (like the Didache) mention alternatives like pouring when immersion wasn’t possible. However, these are not Scripture. They reflect historical practice, not biblical prescription. When church tradition contradicts or departs from Scripture, we should follow the Word. While God is gracious and knows our limitations, our goal should always be to obey His instructions as clearly as we can.

4. Local Church Autonomy and Biblical Faithfulness
Yes, local churches are accountable before God for their practices, but autonomy does not mean license to redefine biblical ordinances. The local church is to follow apostolic teaching (Acts 2:42), and baptism is one of those foundational teachings. It’s not just a symbolic act to be customized—it’s a command to be obeyed as given.

5. Grace and Clarity
I absolutely believe God is gracious in cases of genuine inability but exceptions don’t redefine the rule. Just as a picture of marriage can be distorted without nullifying its importance, the solution is not to alter God’s design but to uphold it, even when real-life imperfections exist.
